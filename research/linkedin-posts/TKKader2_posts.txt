Most first-time founders approach fundraising completely backward.

They spend three months obsessing over a slide deck, cold-spam 500 VCs, and wonder why their inbox is a graveyard.

The reality? Investors do not back pitch decks. They back unstoppable momentum.

When I was building ToutApp, I didn't go to Stanford. I didn't have a massive Silicon Valley network. But I still raised Pre-Seed, Seed, Series A, and Series B rounds from the absolute best investors in the world, including Andreessen Horowitz.

The strategy wasn’t about mastering a pitch. It was about understanding that fundraising is a relationship and momentum game.

Here is the raw reality of how those rounds actually came together:

• The Pre-Seed: I built the first version of ToutApp over a weekend. We started generating revenues right away, so I got myself an office at one of the 1st WeWork buildings in New York City. My first check happened because Adam Neumann stopped my demo, told me to walk down Lafayette Street, and pitch his friend Daniel in the jewelry district right then and there. Daniel called me back the next day saying he was all in to back the company, which officially started my Pre-Seed round.

• The Seed: We went to Demo Day and walked away with exactly ZERO investors. No one understood the "sales engagement" category yet. They didn't understand why Salespeople needed Email Software. Instead of panicking, I just committed to sending monthly investor updates with our progress. Existing investors saw the grit, loved the transparency, and putting in more money.

• The Series A: Sequoia passed on us after a full partner meeting. To make it worse, our office was literally broken into the morning of the presentation. I was devastated. Sitting on a rooftop in SF, I told myself: "Screw it, let's just ship code and close deals." We hit $1M ARR with just 6 people, walked away from an early acquisition offer from HubSpot, and triggered a preemptive Series A from Jackson Square Ventures.

• The Series B: An introduction from an early investor led me to Andreessen Horowitz (a16z). We walked into the room, nailed the presentation, and they led our Series B.

If you are staring at an empty investor pipeline right now, it is incredibly easy to feel like the game is rigged against you if you don't have the "perfect" pedigree.

I used to feel that exact same way.

But here is the ultimate truth: Every single time I faced massive friction in fundraising, I stopped chasing VCs and completely obsessed over my Go-To-Market strategy. When your GTM moves, your metrics improve, and that is what investors actually fall in love with.
